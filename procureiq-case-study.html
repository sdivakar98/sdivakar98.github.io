Overview

The scenario

The Department of Infrastructure and Procurement (DIP) was evaluating ProcureIQ — an AI-assisted supplier evaluation system — for use across standard government contracts valued between $80,000 and $500,000. The system would ingest tender submissions, score them against weighted evaluation criteria, and produce a ranked shortlist for human delegates.

I was engaged to conduct a pre-deployment AI risk assessment to determine whether ProcureIQ was fit for purpose across its five proposed use cases, and to identify the governance controls required before deployment could proceed.

Key Findings

What the assessment found

Critical — Algorithmic bias risk

ProcureIQ's training data (2018–2024 historical awards) likely underrepresents SME and Indigenous suppliers, creating a structural scoring disadvantage that must be independently audited before deployment.

Critical — Explainability gap

The system cannot generate per-submission explainability reports in its current state. Without this, affected suppliers have no basis on which to understand or challenge a score — a breach of emerging accountability standards.

High — Over-reliance risk

Without structured training, procurement delegates are likely to treat AI-ranked shortlists as authoritative rather than advisory — undermining the human oversight requirement under the PGPA Act.

Strength — Sound technical foundation

For lower-stakes pre-screening use cases, ProcureIQ presents a manageable risk profile. The system architecture is sound and the vendor has committed to Australian data residency.

Use Case Decisions

The split outcome

The assessment concluded with a split decision — reflecting that AI risk is context-dependent and proportionate governance requires nuance, not a binary approve/reject.

Use Case Contract Scope Decision Rationale
Supplier pre-screening $80K – $200K Approved Lower stakes, human review at shortlist stage, explainability adequate.
Weighted criteria scoring $80K – $500K Conditional Approved subject to bias testing, audit logging, and explainability controls.
Automated shortlist ranking $200K – $500K Conditional Requires mandatory human review of all ranked outputs before progression.
Final contract recommendation $200K – $500K Not Approved Insufficient explainability and accountability for high-value decisions.
SME / Indigenous supplier scoring All values Not Approved Unacceptable bias risk without dedicated fairness testing framework.
Framework

NIST AI RMF mapping

The assessment was structured around the four core functions of the NIST AI Risk Management Framework:

Govern

Establishes accountability structures for ProcureIQ — including human oversight requirements, procurement delegate accountability, and supplier redress mechanisms.

Map

Identifies and categorises risks in context — including historical bias in training data and the elevated vulnerability of SME and Indigenous supplier cohorts.

Measure

Quantifies risks through pre-deployment bias testing, ongoing model performance monitoring, and per-submission explainability reporting.

Manage

Implements the controls table — 9 controls across immediate, 30-day, and 90-day implementation timelines, with a named AI Risk Owner designated within DIP.

Reflection

Why this project matters

Procurement is one of the highest-stakes applications of AI in the public sector. Errors in supplier evaluation affect commercial outcomes, market competition, and — in the case of SME and Indigenous supplier programs — government policy objectives. Getting it wrong isn't just a technical failure; it's an accountability failure.

This assessment demonstrates the kind of thinking AI governance professionals need to bring: not just identifying that risks exist, but understanding who is harmed, why the harm matters in this specific context, and what proportionate controls look like. The split decision — approving some use cases while blocking others — reflects that responsible AI governance is rarely binary.

My forensic background directly informed this assessment. The questions I asked of ProcureIQ are the same questions I ask of any system under investigation: can you explain your outputs, can you prove they're reliable, and what happens when they're wrong?
